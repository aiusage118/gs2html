Parliamentary privileges

About

  • Parliamentary privileges are certain rights and immunities enjoyed by members of Parliament, individually and collectively, so that they can "effectively discharge their functions".
  • emerged in British House of Commons when nascent British Parliament started to protect its sovereignty from excesses of monarch
  • No one law of codification but are based on five sources: i) Constitutional provisions ii) Various laws of parliament (iii) Rules of both houses iv) Parliamentary conventions v) Judicial interpretations.

Article 105 And 194 of confer certain privileges on mem and house

  • Individual
    • No arrest during session and 40 days before and 40 days after session.
    • Not liable in court for any speech in parliament
    • Exempted from jury service when house is in session (SAJ)
  • Collective
    • Exclude strangers from proceedings. Hold secret sitting of legislature
    • Freedom of press to publish true reports of Parliamentary proceedings.
    • Right to punish members and outsiders for breach of its privileges:
    • Only Parliament can make rules to regulate its own proceedings
    • There is a bar on court from making inquiry into proceedings of house Art 122

  • Motion by member, acceptance by speaker, inquiry by committee, deliberation by house, action by speaker

Parliamentary Privileges (Reference: Articles 105, 194)

Cases related to Breach of Privilege:

  • Against Smriti Irani on Rohith Vemula case.
  • 1978, Indira Gandhi faced it on basis of observations of excesses during emergency (Justice Shah Committee report). Subsequently, she was expelled from house.
  • Expulsion of Subramanyam Swamy from RS in 1976 for bringing disrepute to Parliament.
  • Karnataka assembly passed a resolution imposing imprisonment and fines on scribes in 2017.
  • In RS against CM of Kerala after Kerala Assembly passed a resolution against CAA.

Importance of Privileges:

  • Protect freedom of speech and expression in House and insulates them against litigation over matters that occur in these houses.
  • Ensure their functioning without undue influence, pressure or coercion.
  • Ensure sovereignty of Parliament.
  • Protect against any libel through speeches, printing or publishing.

Issues with Privileges (Against 'Constitutional philosophy'):

  • Doctrine of limited powers: Absence of codified privileges gives unbridled power to house to decide when and how breach of privilege occurs.
  • Doctrine of separation of power: Speaker acts as complainant, advocate and the judge. Used as a substitute for legal proceedings.
  • Doctrine of judicial review: Judicial scrutiny is barred in cases of privileges (Art 122).
  • Violate FR of speech of expression: Breach of privilege invoked for genuine criticism of members of house or due to political vendetta, reduces accountability of elected representatives.
    • It often comes in conflict with FR of people and press. Karnataka case - Journalists.
  • Decisions of the speaker may be influenced by his/her political affiliations. Like KN, manipur, AP.
  • Invoked on grounds of defamation by individual members, while judicial remedy available under defamation and libel law.

Suggestion for Privileges:

Recommendations of National Commission to Review Working of Constitution (NCRWC) + 2nd ARC:

  • Privileges of legislators should be defined and delimited and codified to remove arbitrariness.
    • Like Australia passed Parliamentary Privileges Act in 1987. ✔️
    • Defining privileges, conditions of their breach and penalties.
  • Trial must be conducted by a competent, independent and impartial tribunal. ✔️
  • Find balance: 'Sovereign people of India' have restricted right to free speech while 'their representatives' have absolute freedom of speech in houses. Courts must revisit earlier judgments to find right balance between Fundamental Rights of the citizens and privileges of legislature.
  • Judiciary must set limits on punitive powers.
    • Penal action is unwarranted, unless attempt to obstruct functioning of house or its members.
  • Must only be invoked by legislature when there is "real obstruction to its functioning".

  8. Making finance Committees' reports public.
  9. Increase power of ethics, proper code of conduct due to emerging issue.

Strengthening the committee system can go a long way in improving the quality of laws drafted and minimise potential implementation challenges.

  • Decide who will speak for how long and which part of speech will not go on record.
  • Guards the rights and privileges of the members.
  • Immense powers of interpreting Rules of Procedure.
Major implications (need for neutrality):

  • Member of Selection Committees of important statutory bodies - CVC, CIC, NHRC, Lokpal and non-statutory - CBI.
  • Committees of parliament function essentially under the Speaker & their chairpersons are also appointed or nominated by him. If the Speaker is a member of any committee, Ex-officio chairman of such a committee. (Eg. Rules Committee, Business Advisory Committee, General Purposes Committee)

National Court of Appeal (Art 130, P Vasantha Kumar case)

National Court of Appeal, with regional benches in Chennai, Mumbai and Kolkata, is meant to act as final courts of justice, appeals from HC and tribunals, bifurcate higher judiciary, with SC exclusively hearing constitutional and public law cases.

    • Equal access to justice for all is a fundamental right under Constitution.
  • Increase access and affordability: Reduce cost of litigation and would enable litigants to have services of lawyer who appeared for them before HC.
  • Functions of Supreme Court as a Constitutional Court have been seriously impaired.
    • In 1960s it would decide over 100 such cases a year. But now fewer than 8 Constitution benches a year.
    • Number of judges required to hear cases of constitutional importance are less then required, which often means lesson authority and Deliberation by less no. Of judges.
    • Supreme Court was never intended to be regular court of appeal, created for laying down law for entire country.
  • High Courts can and do make serious mistakes, sometimes egregious ones, and these require rectification.
    • Forces Supreme Court to take on such appeals.
    • Answer is to create intermediate courts of appeal where senior judges can deal with these cases, giving them time and consideration they deserve.
  • 229th report of Law Commission of India.
  • Retaining New Delhi bench of SC as a Constitutional Court and establishment of Cassation Benches of Supreme Court in four regions at New Delhi, Chennai/Hyderabad, Kolkata and Mumbai.
  • Since Article 130 of Constitution, says CJI can make with approval of pres., would require no constitutional amendment.
  • This basic model with appropriate variations has worked very successfully in countries such as Italy, Egypt, Ireland, U.S. and Denmark.
  • If we do not do something because it has never been done before, we will go nowhere. Law will stagnate while society advances, which is not good for both.

Issues with tribunalisation: (of Justice) (Qualif., dominated by exec, vacancies, Independence, conflict of interest, Opacity)

  • Against the separation of powers: Tribunalisation is seen as encroachment on judicial branch by the government.
  • Despite playing the justice delivery role, administrative responsibility for tribunals is typically with the ministries of the government that administer the statutes under which the tribunals are created.
  • Appeal: originally set up to provide specialized justice delivery and to reduce the burden on regular courts. However, appeals from tribunals have inevitably managed to enter the mainstream judicial system. (L. Chandra Kumar case)
  • High Pendency: lack of adequate infrastructure to work smoothly and perform the functions originally envisioned defeating the purpose of their creation.
  • ✓ Appointments: under executive control. Executive also determines staffing.
  • There is a lack of information available on the functioning of tribunals. Websites are non-existent, unresponsive or not updated.
  • Accessibility is low due to scant geographic availability therefore justice becomes expensive and difficult.

Lokpal and Lokayukta Act, 2013

  • Institutional mechanism: Establishment of Lokpal for Union and Lokayukta for States to inquire into allegations of corruption against certain public functionaries.
  • Composition: Lokpal will consist of a chairperson and a maximum of 8 members, of which 50% judicial members and 50% from SC/ST/OBCs, minorities and women.
  • Appointment process: Two-stage process.
    • Search committee which recommends a panel of names to high-power selection committee.
    • Selection committee comprises of PM, LOP, Speaker, CJI or (his nominee) and eminent jurist (nominated by President based on recommendation of other members of the panel).
  • Jurisdiction:
    • Anyone PM, minister, officials of Union government under Groups A, B, C and D.
    • Chairpersons, members, officers and directors of any board, corporation, society, trust or established by Act of Parliament or wholly or partly funded by Centre.
    • Any society or trust or body receives foreign contribution above ₹10 lakh.
  • Exception for Prime Minister:
    • Does Not allow Lokpal inquiry if allegation against PM relates to IR, external and internal security, public order, atomic energy and space.
    • Complaints not to be probed unless full Lokpal bench considers initiate inquiry + 2/3rds of members approve it.
    • Such inquiry to be held in camera + if Lokpal conclude to dismiss complain records not to be published.
  • Operating mechanism:
    • Inquiry Wing for conducting preliminary inquiry.
    • Prosecution Wing for prosecution of public servants on complaint by Lokpal under this Act.
    • Timelines for enquiry, investigation: 60 days for completion of inquiry and 6 months for completion of investigation by CBI. 6 months extension by Lokpal on a written request from CBI.
    • Power of superintendence and direction over any investigation agency including CBI for cases referred to them by Lokpal.
    • Special Court shall be setup to hear and decide the cases referred by Lokpal.
    • Provisions for attachment and confiscation of property acquired by corrupt means, even while prosecution is pending.
  • Measure for independence:
    • Administrative expenses of Lokpal, including salaries, allowances and pensions be charged upon CFI.
    • Suspension, removal: Chairperson or Member be removed by order of President on grounds of misbehaviour after SC report. For that a petition has to be signed by at least 100 MPs.
    • Salaries, allowances and service conditions of Lokpal chairperson be same as CJI; for other members same as judge of SC.

Positive features of the Act:

  • Wide jurisdiction including PM. (Karnataka lokayukta. 'Suo motu action for desecration of Hulimavu lake bund - houses inundated.)
    • Applicable to public servants in and outside India. has extraterritorial operation.
  • Empowers citizens to complain to Lokpal against corruption by public officials,+ hold authorities accountable.
  • Special courts and clear timelines at each stage can ensure timeliness.
  • Provisions for prosecution and punishment for filing false complaint ensure that it is not misused for political gains or to settle other scores.
  • Lokpal can issue directions to agencies in India like CBI, CVC while investigating, and prosecuting case under direction of Lokpal, ensures independent functioning free from government interference.
  • Attempt to protect identity of complainant.

Issues with the Act:

  • Limited power of lokpal. (Issues - Lack of power, Implementation, Anti Whistleblower features.) (Types: Whistleblower, Anon not allowed, Heavy penalty)
  • Requirement of Government Approval: NO power of prior sanction with Lokpal for enquiry and investigation of government officials.
  • Time Frame limitation: cant inquire into complain after 7 years from date of offense, restrict scope especially in relation to some of large scams are exposed from time to time.
  • No authority with lokpal to take Suo Moto cognisance of cases of corruption and maladministration.
  • Exclusion of judiciary from ambit of Lokpal. (PM related)

Problems in implementation mechanism:

  • Constitution of Lokayukta: mandates establishment of Lokayukta in every state within 1 year from date of commencement of this Act. However, many states havent done this (16).

Legislative Council

Odisha Legislative Assembly for the formation of the State Legislative Council. Andhra Pradesh is mulling the abolition of the second chamber.

Base For Formation:

  • India has a bicameral system of legislature. Just as Parliament has two Houses, the states can also have a Legislative Council in addition to the Legislative Assembly through Article 169 (both abolition and creation) of the Constitution.
  • Six States having a Legislative Council: Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Maharashtra, Karnataka.

Article 169 - The Parliament can abolish a legislative council or create it by a simple majority, if the legislative assembly of the concerned state, by a special majority, passes a resolution to that effect.

Composition (PT type):

  • Under Article 171 of the Constitution, the Legislative Council of a state shall not have more than one-third-of the total strength of the State Assembly, and not less than 40 members.
  • Like the Rajya Sabha, the legislative council is a continuing chamber, that is, it is a permanent body and is not subject to dissolution. The tenure of a Member of the Legislative Council (MLC) is six years, with one-third of the members retiring every two years.

Manner of Election:

  • One-third of the MLCs are elected by the state's MLAs,
  • Another 1/3rd by a special electorate comprising sitting members of local governments such as municipalities and district boards,
  • 1/12th by an electorate of teachers and another 1/12th by registered graduates.
  • The remaining members are appointed by the Governor for distinguished services in various fields namely, literature, science, art, cooperative movement and social service.

LC vs Rajya Sabha:

  • The legislative power of the Councils are limited. Unlike Rajya Sabha which has substantial powers to shape non-financial legislation, Legislative Councils lack a constitutional mandate to do so.
  • Assemblies can override suggestions/amendments made to legislation by the Council.
  • Again, unlike Rajya Sabha MPs, MLCs cannot vote in elections for the President and Vice President. The Vice President is the Rajya Sabha Chairperson while a member from the Council itself is chosen as the Council Chairperson.

Arguments in Favour:

  • A Legislative Council can help check hasty actions by the directly elected House.
  • The Legislative Council also enables non-elected individuals to contribute to the legislative process.

Arguments Against:

  • Legislative Council can delay legislation.
  • It can also be used to park leaders who have not been able to win an election.
  • Faaltu burden on exchequer.
